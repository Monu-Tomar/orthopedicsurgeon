Common Causes of Shoulder Pain

Shoulder pain can stem from various conditions, injuries, or wear and tear affecting the joint, muscles, or tendons. Common causes include:

Rotator Cuff Injuries

Tears or inflammation in the tendons around the shoulder joint, often from overuse or injury, leading to pain and weakness.

Frozen Shoulder

Also known as adhesive capsulitis, this condition involves stiffness and limited movement in the shoulder due to inflammation and tissue tightening.

Shoulder Impingement

Occurs when the shoulder tendons are pinched during movement, commonly seen in repetitive overhead activities, causing pain and reduced range of motion.

Arthritis

Osteoarthritis or rheumatoid arthritis in the shoulder joint can cause chronic pain, swelling, and stiffness.

Shoulder Dislocation

A shoulder dislocation occurs when the upper arm bone slips out of the shoulder socket, often resulting from trauma or structural weakness.

Bursitis

Inflammation of the bursa (small, fluid-filled sacs) in the shoulder joint, leading to pain, especially with movement.

Symptoms of Shoulder Pain

Aching or Sharp Pain
Pain can range from a constant dull ache to sharp, sudden pain, especially with specific movements.
Limited Range of Motion
Difficulty moving the shoulder fully, such as raising or rotating the arm.
Weakness in the Shoulder
Weakness or inability to lift or hold objects, often associated with rotator cuff injuries.
Swelling or Tenderness
The shoulder area may appear swollen or feel tender to the touch, particularly around the joint.
Clicking or Popping Sounds
Sensations of clicking, popping, or grinding in the shoulder during movement.
Pain When Sleeping or Lying on the Shoulder
Increased discomfort at night or while lying on the affected side.

When to See a Doctor for Shoulder Pain?

Consider seeing a doctor if you experience any of the following:

Persistent Pain Lasting More Than a Few Days

Shoulder pain that doesn’t improve with rest or home care may require medical evaluation.

Difficulty Moving the Shoulder

Limited range of motion, stiffness, or inability to fully raise or rotate the arm may indicate structural issues.

Shoulder Pain Following an Injury or Fall

Pain after trauma, such as a fall or sports injury, should be assessed for potential fractures, dislocations, or tears.

Swelling, Redness, or Warmth

Signs of inflammation or infection, such as swelling or warmth, should be evaluated promptly.

Weakness or Instability

A shoulder that feels weak, unstable, or prone to "giving way" could indicate ligament or tendon damage.

Pain When Sleeping

Shoulder pain that interferes with sleep, especially if it worsens when lying on the affected side, should be examined.

FAQs About Shoulder Pain Doctor- Ranchi

Can shoulder pain be treated without surgery?
Yes, many cases are effectively managed with physical therapy, medications, and lifestyle changes without needing surgery.
What is shoulder arthroscopy?
Shoulder arthroscopy is a minimally invasive procedure using small incisions and a camera to treat conditions like rotator cuff tears and impingement.
How long does recovery take after shoulder surgery?
Recovery varies but typically takes several weeks to months, depending on the surgery type. Physical therapy aids in faster recovery.
Are injections effective for shoulder pain?
Yes, corticosteroid and PRP injections can reduce inflammation and provide pain relief, particularly for arthritis and bursitis.
What exercises can help relieve shoulder pain?
Strengthening and stretching exercises can relieve pain, but it’s best to consult a physical therapist to ensure safe exercises.
Is shoulder pain a sign of arthritis?
Shoulder pain can result from arthritis, especially if accompanied by stiffness and swelling. Diagnosis is essential to confirm.
Can shoulder pain resolve on its own?
Mild shoulder pain may improve with rest, but if pain persists or worsens, it should be evaluated by a specialist.
