Frozen Shoulder Treatment in Ranchi: Expert Care for Shoulder Pain and Mobility Issues

Frozen shoulder, also known as adhesive capsulitis, is a condition that causes pain and stiffness in the shoulder joint, leading to a significant limitation of movement. Arthroscopy for frozen shoulder is an advanced, minimally invasive procedure used to treat this condition effectively. If conservative treatments such as physical therapy and medications fail to provide relief, an arthroscopic procedure for frozen shoulder can offer significant improvement in mobility and pain reduction.

What is Frozen Shoulder?

A condition where the connective tissue around the shoulder joint becomes inflamed and stiff, making it difficult to move the arm. Over time, the shoulder joint becomes frozen, causing significant pain and restricting motion. The exact cause of frozen shoulder is not always clear, but it is often linked to trauma, surgeries, or underlying health conditions such as diabetes. The condition typically develops in three stages:

  1. Freezing phase: Pain gradually worsens, and shoulder movement becomes limited.
  2. Frozen phase: The pain may decrease, but stiffness continues, making the shoulder almost immovable.
  3. Thawing phase: Gradual recovery of motion and reduction of stiffness.

If non-invasive treatments fail, arthroscopy is often recommended as a solution to restore shoulder mobility and alleviate pain.

The Role of Arthroscopy in Frozen Shoulder Treatment

Arthroscopy involves using a small camera, known as an arthroscope, to view the shoulder joint and guide the surgeon in performing the procedure. This minimally invasive technique is preferred over traditional open surgery due to its reduced risk of complications, smaller incisions, and quicker recovery times. During the procedure, the surgeon can remove inflamed tissue, release tight ligaments, and smooth rough surfaces in the joint to improve mobility.

Benefits of Arthroscopic Procedure

  1. Minimally Invasive: Arthroscopic procedure requires only small incisions, reducing the chances of infection and scarring..
  2. Faster Recovery: Patients experience quicker recovery times, as the procedure minimizes tissue damage and allows for faster rehabilitation.
  3. Pain Relief: The removal of inflamed tissue and the release of tight ligaments provide significant pain relief, leading to improved quality of life.
  4. Improved Range of Motion: By restoring proper movement in the shoulder joint, patients can regain full or near-full mobility.

Diagnosis and Surgery

Diagnosing frozen shoulder begins with a comprehensive assessment by an orthopedic specialist. Diagnosis and surgery process typically includes:

  1. Medical History Review: The doctor will ask about your symptoms, previous shoulder injuries, or surgeries.
  2. Physical Examination: The doctor will evaluate the shoulder‚Äôs range of motion and assess the pain levels.
  3. Imaging Tests: X-rays or MRIs may be used to rule out other conditions, such as rotator cuff tears, and confirm the diagnosis of frozen shoulder.

Once frozen shoulder is diagnosed and conservative treatments like physical therapy and medications have been ineffective, arthroscopy for frozen shoulder becomes a viable surgical option.

What to Expect During Arthroscopy

The arthroscopic procedure for frozen shoulder is performed under general anesthesia or regional anesthesia, depending on the patient‚Äôs condition and the surgeon‚Äôs recommendation. During the procedure:

  1. Small Incisions: The surgeon makes small incisions around the shoulder joint to insert the arthroscope and surgical instruments.
  2. Removal of Damaged Tissue: The surgeon removes or trims inflamed tissue and tight ligaments that contribute to the stiffness.
  3. Joint Manipulation: In some cases, the surgeon may perform a ‚Äúcapsular release,‚Äù where they manipulate the shoulder joint to help break up adhesions and restore normal range of motion.
  4. Completion: The procedure typically lasts between 30 minutes to an hour, and patients are generally sent home the same day.

The benefits of an arthroscopic procedure for frozen shoulder are clear, as it is less invasive than traditional surgery and comes with fewer risks, such as infection and excessive bleeding.

Recovery After Surgery

Recovery after arthroscopy for frozen shoulder is typically faster compared to open surgery. The recovery timeline varies based on the severity of the condition and the patient‚Äôs commitment to the rehabilitation process. Here‚Äôs what patients can expect post-surgery:

  1. Postoperative Care: A sling may be required for a few days to support the shoulder and limit movement.
  2. Physical Therapy: Physical therapy is crucial for recovery, as it helps regain strength, mobility, and flexibility in the shoulder joint. The physical therapist will lead the patient through exercises aimed at gradually improving the range of motion.
  3. Pain Management: Mild pain and swelling are normal, and over-the-counter pain medications can help manage discomfort.
  4. Return to Activities: Patients can usually resume light activities within 4-6 weeks, with complete recovery taking approximately 3-6 months.

Patients who follow their surgeon‚Äôs recommendations and commit to rehabilitation often see significant improvements in shoulder function.
